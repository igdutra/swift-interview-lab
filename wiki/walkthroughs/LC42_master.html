LC 42 — Trapping Rain Water

Given an elevation map — an array of non-negative integers where each value is the height of a vertical bar of width 1 — compute how much rainwater can be trapped between the bars after it rains.

InputOutputWhy
[0,1,0,2,1,0,1,3,2,1,2,1]6Six units trapped in the valleys between the bars
[4,2,0,3,2,5]9Large walls on both ends trap water above the inner bars
[3,0,3]3Minimal valley: 3 units trapped between two equal walls

The twist that makes it Hard: water above any bar depends on both its left and right neighbors simultaneously — not just one direction. You cannot slide a fixed-width window; the walls on each side of any position can be arbitrarily far away, and you need the tallest bar on each side, not just an adjacent one. The elegant solution encodes both sides at once with two running maxima that close in from opposite ends.

2   Reading the Problem — insights from the statement

"Elevation map" (array of heights): each index is a position on a number line; each value is bar height. Water fills the space between bars — it can only be trapped if there is a taller bar on both the left and right. A bar at the edge of the array can never trap water because one side is open.
"How much water": you need a total, not a boolean. Water above bar i is exactly min(maxWallLeft, maxWallRight) − height[i] — the shorter wall is the ceiling; anything above it spills over. Sum this across every bar to get the answer. This formula is the problem's core mechanic.
"Between the bars" — the sliding-window trap: it is tempting to reach for a sliding window because the problem involves a contiguous array and asks for a cumulative sum. The trap: a sliding window processes one direction at a time, but water at any position is determined by the maximum on both sides independently. There is no fixed window width to slide — the bounding walls can be anywhere. This problem is about prefix/suffix maxima, not a contiguous range of a fixed or expanding size.
0 ≤ height[i] ≤ 10⁵: heights are non-negative. A bar of height 0 is a flat gap — it can trap water if both neighbors are taller. height[i] is never negative, so min(maxWallLeft, maxWallRight) − height[i] is always ≥ 0 — you never subtract trapped water.

4   Try It Yourself First

Attempt the problem before reading on. Open hints one at a time.

Hint 1 — what determines water at one bar?

Water above bar i depends on both its tallest left neighbor and tallest right neighbor. Write down the formula. What do you need to know at every position?

Hint 2 — the O(n) space approach

Precompute two arrays: prefixMax[i] = max height from index 0 to i, and suffixMax[i] = max height from i to n−1. Then water[i] = min(prefixMax[i], suffixMax[i]) − height[i]. Can you do this with O(1) space?

Hint 3 — eliminating the arrays

Start with two pointers at opposite ends, each tracking a running max. If maxWallLeft < maxWallRight, the left wall is the bottleneck — you already know the right side is taller, so it's safe to commit the water at the left pointer right now. You don't need to know exactly how tall the right wall is — only that it's taller. Advance the left pointer and repeat.

5   Interview Simulation

The interviewer has stated the problem. The script starts with your first words — practice it out loud.

Step 1 — Clarifying questions
Q: "Can heights be zero? Is a bar of height 0 just a flat gap in the ground?"
A: Yes, heights can be 0. → A zero-height bar can still trap water if both neighbors are taller. The formula still holds: min(maxLeft, maxRight) − 0.
Q: "The bars at the very edges of the array — can they trap water?"
A: No — the outermost bars are always walls, never interior. → I'll seed my running maxima with the outermost bar heights before the loop, so the loop only processes interior positions.
Q: "Is this a sliding window problem? It feels like it involves a contiguous range…"
A: No. A sliding window computes something over a contiguous subarray of fixed or growing width — it only looks in one direction. Water above bar i requires the maximum bar on both the left AND right independently, and those walls can be anywhere. There is no fixed window to slide. → This needs prefix-max and suffix-max information simultaneously.
Q: "Is O(n) time and O(1) space the target?"
A: Yes, ideal. → This confirms the two-pointer approach over the O(n) space prefix-array version.
Step 2 — The plan, before any code
The plan, spoken

"This is a two-pointer problem — specifically the left/right-max accumulator variant. The signal is: water at any position depends on both the tallest bar to its left and the tallest bar to its right. That dual dependency is the key: I can't solve it with a single pass in one direction."

"Let me pin the conditions first. The governing formula is: water at bar i = min(maxWallLeft, maxWallRight) − height[i]. The validity condition for committing water at a position: I must know that the shorter of the two walls is already the bottleneck — that no future bar on that side will lower the ceiling I just computed. That is exactly what the two-pointer closing invariant gives me."

"The insight: if maxWallLeft is less than maxWallRight, the left wall is the bottleneck at the left pointer's current position. The right wall is already taller — it doesn't matter how tall exactly, because even the shortest future right wall will still be taller than maxWallLeft. So I can commit the water at the left pointer right now, advance it inward, and update maxWallLeft. Mirror logic for the right side when maxWallRight is smaller or equal."

"The data structures are minimal: two integer pointers closing from both ends, two running max integers — one per side — and a result accumulator. No arrays, no sets, no maps. I seed maxWallLeft and maxWallRight from the outermost bar heights so the loop only processes interior positions — the outermost bars are walls, not water."

"The walk: initialize leftPointer=0, rightPointer=n−1, maxWallLeft=height[0], maxWallRight=height[n−1], totalWater=0. Loop while leftPointer < rightPointer. If maxWallLeft < maxWallRight: advance leftPointer, update maxWallLeft, add maxWallLeft − height[leftPointer] to totalWater. Otherwise: advance rightPointer, update maxWallRight, add maxWallRight − height[rightPointer]. Return totalWater."

"Expected O(n) time — each bar visited at most once — and O(1) space — four scalar variables. I'll trace the canonical example to verify."

6   Why This Approach — and not the others

CandidateCostVerdict
Brute force: for each bar, scan left and right to find maxO(n²) time, O(1) spaceWorks but rescans every bar twice per position — wasteful; n=2×10⁴ → 4×10⁸ ops, borderline
Sliding window—Wrong tool. Water needs max on both sides simultaneously; a window only looks in one direction. There is no fixed or expanding range to slide. This is the most common wrong instinct — name it explicitly to show you know the difference.
Prefix-max arrays (O(n) space)O(n) time, O(n) spaceCorrect and readable: precompute prefixMax[i] and suffixMax[i], then one final loop. Easier to reason about, good stepping stone to understanding the two-pointer version.
Converging two pointers — left/right-max accumulatorsO(n) time, O(1) spaceChosen. Space-optimized version of the prefix-array approach: instead of storing full arrays, maintain running maxima on each side. Commit water on the bottleneck side, advance inward. Each bar visited exactly once.
THE ONE IDEA: You don't need to know the exact height of the taller wall — you only need to know it's taller. When maxWallLeft < maxWallRight, the left wall is the bottleneck. Whatever is between leftPointer and rightPointer on the right side, it doesn't matter — the right wall is already guaranteed to be taller than the left. So you can commit the water at the left pointer right now. This is what makes the two-pointer version correct even though it never looks at the middle of the array explicitly: the closing invariant — "the other side is always taller" — is maintained at every step.
When to reach for the left/right-max accumulator pattern: whenever a per-position result depends on the maximum (or minimum) seen to the left AND to the right simultaneously, and you want O(1) space. The prefix-array version is the clearer starting point; the two-pointer version is its O(1) space compression.
PITFALLS
▸Reaching for sliding window. Sliding window processes one direction at a time. Water depends on both sides — this is not a sliding window problem. If you find yourself trying to define what "valid" means for a window here, stop: there is no such condition, because there is no window.
▸Initializing maxWallLeft and maxWallRight to 0. If both start at 0, the first iteration processes the outermost bars as interior positions and adds water to them — but those bars are walls. Seed from height[0] and height[n−1] so the loop starts with pointers already past the outermost bars.
▸Advancing the pointer before updating the running max. Update maxWallLeft/maxWallRight before computing water at the new position, or the max won't include the bar you just moved to.

7   Data Structures — choice and justification

VariableHoldsWhy this and not something else
leftPointer: IntCurrent position advancing from the leftOne integer replaces an entire left-side prefix-max array — the running max is sufficient because we only ever need the max seen so far, not the full history
rightPointer: IntCurrent position advancing from the rightSame reasoning — replaces the suffix-max array
maxWallLeft: IntTallest bar seen so far from the left, seeded at height[0]Seeding from the outermost bar ensures the loop starts with a valid wall; initializing to 0 would incorrectly treat the first bar as interior
maxWallRight: IntTallest bar seen so far from the right, seeded at height[n−1]Same reasoning as maxWallLeft
totalWater: IntAccumulated trapped waterA plain integer accumulator — never negative because max(wall) ≥ height[i] always
The prefix-array connection: maxWallLeft at any step equals what prefixMax[leftPointer] would contain in the O(n) space version. maxWallRight equals suffixMax[rightPointer]. The two-pointer version computes the same values on the fly, discarding what it no longer needs.

8   Pseudocode Dry Run

Pseudocodeleft/right-max accumulator — two-pointer approach
leftPointer  ← 0
rightPointer ← n − 1
maxWallLeft  ← height[0]       // seed from outermost bars — they are walls, not water
maxWallRight ← height[n − 1]
totalWater   ← 0

WHILE leftPointer < rightPointer:
    IF maxWallLeft < maxWallRight:
        // Left wall is the bottleneck. Right is already taller — doesn't matter how much.
        // Safe to commit water at left pointer now.
        leftPointer += 1
        maxWallLeft ← MAX(maxWallLeft, height[leftPointer])
        totalWater  += maxWallLeft − height[leftPointer]
    ELSE:
        // Right wall is the bottleneck (or tie — same logic applies).
        rightPointer -= 1
        maxWallRight ← MAX(maxWallRight, height[rightPointer])
        totalWater   += maxWallRight − height[rightPointer]

RETURN totalWater

9   Swift Solution

A.1 Converging two pointers — left/right-max accumulators
O(n)O(1)
Plan
Seed two running maxima from the outermost bar heights. Close two pointers inward. At each step, process the side with the shorter wall — that wall is the bottleneck, and the other side is already guaranteed taller, so committing water on the bottleneck side is safe. Update the running max before computing water so it includes the bar just moved to. 
func trap(_ height: [Int]) -> Int {
    var leftPointer  = 0
    var rightPointer = height.count - 1
    // Seed from outermost bars — they are walls, never interior water positions.
    var maxWallLeft  = height[leftPointer]
    var maxWallRight = height[rightPointer]
    var totalWater   = 0

    while leftPointer < rightPointer {
        if maxWallLeft < maxWallRight {
            // Left wall is the bottleneck. Right is already taller —
            // water at leftPointer cannot leak right, so commit it now.
            leftPointer += 1
            maxWallLeft = max(maxWallLeft, height[leftPointer])
            let waterTrapped = maxWallLeft - height[leftPointer]
            totalWater += waterTrapped
        } else {
            // Right wall is the bottleneck (or tie — same logic applies).
            // Left is already taller, so commit water at rightPointer now.
            rightPointer -= 1
            maxWallRight = max(maxWallRight, height[rightPointer])
            let waterTrapped = maxWallRight - height[rightPointer]
            totalWater += waterTrapped
        }
    }

    return totalWater
}

11   Question → Code Map

Question / decisionCode it became
"What determines water above bar i?"let waterTrapped = maxWallLeft - height[leftPointer] (or right variant)
"Which side is the bottleneck?"if maxWallLeft < maxWallRight — the shorter wall is always the ceiling
"When is it safe to commit water without knowing the other side?"Always — when one wall is shorter, the taller wall guarantees no spill on that side
"How do I avoid treating outermost bars as water positions?"var maxWallLeft = height[leftPointer] — seed before the loop; loop advances first
"Which pointer advances?"The one on the bottleneck side — leftPointer += 1 when left is shorter, rightPointer -= 1 when right is shorter or tied
"When does the loop end?"while leftPointer < rightPointer — when they meet, no interior position remains

13   Follow-Up Bank

Follow-up: "Before you push this to production — can you see any improvements you'd make?"

The solution is already optimal in time and space — O(n) / O(1). One constant-factor note: the two branches are symmetric mirror images. In a production codebase I might extract a helper to reduce duplication, but the asymmetry between left and right is the core logic, so keeping it explicit actually aids readability. No algorithmic improvement is possible.

Follow-up: "Can you solve this with O(n) space instead — what would that look like?"

Yes — the prefix-max arrays approach. Precompute prefixMax[i] = max(height[0..i]) and suffixMax[i] = max(height[i..n−1]) in two passes, then a third pass computes water[i] = min(prefixMax[i], suffixMax[i]) − height[i]. This is easier to reason about and a good stepping stone to understanding why the two-pointer version is correct.

Follow-up: "What if the bars have non-integer heights — floats?"

The algorithm is identical — only the type changes from Int to Double. The formula min(maxWallLeft, maxWallRight) − height[i] and the accumulation are the same. No structural change needed.

Follow-up: "What if instead of 1D bars you had a 2D elevation map — a matrix of heights?"

This is LC 407 — Trapping Rain Water II. The 2D version requires a min-heap (priority queue) seeded with the border cells, then a BFS-like expansion inward — always processing the shortest border cell next. The same invariant applies: the shortest cell on the current frontier is the bottleneck. The two-pointer trick doesn't directly generalize to 2D because there are more than two sides.
