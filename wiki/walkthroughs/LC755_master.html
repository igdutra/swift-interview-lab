Theory
text_formatting_deep_dive_master.html

You are given an elevation map as an array heights, where heights[i] is the height of the terrain at column i. Then volume units of water are dropped, one at a time, from above a fixed column index K. Each unit of water falls onto column K and then flows according to gravity until it settles. After all the water has been poured, return the final heights array (terrain plus settled water).

InputOutputWhy
heights=[2,1,1,2,1,2,2], volume=4, K=3[2,2,2,3,2,2,2]Water at K=3 flows to the lower valleys on each side, filling them before stacking at K
heights=[1,2,3,4], volume=2, K=2[2,3,3,4]From K=2 water can only flow left (downhill); both units settle at index 0, raising it twice
heights=[3,1,3], volume=5, K=1[4,4,4]The central valley fills, then water stacks at K once both sides are level

The twist is entirely in the settle rule for a single drop: water prefers to flow to the lowest reachable position, it prefers left over right when both are equally low, and it can never reach a low spot that lies behind a taller wall. Get those three rules and their precedence right and the problem is solved; get any one subtly wrong and you pass the simple cases and fail the symmetric ones.

2   Reading the Problem — insights from the constraints

"Water is dropped one unit at a time": the volume units do not settle simultaneously — each unit settles completely before the next is dropped, and it settles against the terrain as modified by all the earlier units. So the state you mutate is the heights array itself, and each drop is an independent local search over the current heights.
"Flows according to gravity": a unit at column K tries to move to an adjacent lower column, repeating until it can't go lower. This is not a global minimum search — it is a reachable minimum: water can only descend or stay level on its way, never climb over a peak to reach a lower valley on the far side.
The settle precedence is fixed: left, then right, then in place. A unit first tries to flow as far left as it can to the lowest reachable position. If there is no lower position to the left (the path is blocked or only rises), it tries the same to the right. If neither side offers a lower reachable spot, it stays at K and raises heights[K] by 1. This ordering is the source of the left-bias tie-break.
"Lowest reachable point on a side": scanning left from K, you track the lowest column you can reach without the terrain first rising above your current path. The moment a column is taller than where you currently stand, the wall blocks you — everything beyond it is unreachable on that side, and you settle at the lowest point found before the wall.
Constraints are small: width up to ~100 and volume up to ~100. An O(volume · width) simulation — for each drop, an outward scan of up to the full width — is comfortably fast. There is no pressure to be clever; the entire difficulty is the correctness of the inner settle search and its tie-breaks.

4   Clarifying Questions

The questions to ask before you start — each answer's design consequence is noted. (No spoken plan and no code on this page: this is an ATTEMPT.)

Clarifying questions
Q: "When water can flow equally low to the left and to the right, which side does it pick?"
A: Left. → My settle search must try the left side fully before the right; a symmetric valley sends water left.
Q: "Can water flow over a taller column to reach a lower valley on the far side?"
A: No — it can only descend (or stay level) on the way. → The inner scan stops at the first column taller than my current footing; reachable, not global, minimum.
Q: "Does each unit settle fully before the next is dropped, against the updated terrain?"
A: Yes. → I mutate the heights array in place and re-scan from K for every one of the volume drops.
Q: "Is K always a valid in-range index, and are heights non-negative?"
A: Yes, K is in range and heights are non-negative integers. → No validation needed, but my inner scan still needs lower/upper bound guards so it doesn't walk off the array.

6   Try It Yourself First

Solve it before peeking. Open the hints one at a time — each reveals only one more layer.

Hint 1 — the constraint

The units fall one at a time and each settles against the terrain left by the previous ones. With width and volume both small, you can afford to fully re-simulate each single drop from scratch. So the shape is: an outer loop running volume times, and inside it, "settle exactly one unit."

Hint 2 — the settle rule, in order

For one unit dropped at K, try three things in this strict order. (1) Flow left: scan leftward from K, remembering the lowest reachable index, stopping the instant the terrain rises above your current footing. If a strictly lower index was found, settle there. (2) Otherwise flow right by the same rule. (3) Otherwise stay at K. Whichever index wins, increment its height by 1.

Hint 3 — the reachability detail that breaks naive solutions

"Lowest reachable" is not "lowest overall." As you scan left, keep a bestIndex initialized to K and a running "current level." Move one column at a time: if the next column is higher than the current level, stop — it's a wall. If it is lower than heights[bestIndex], update bestIndex. This way a low valley hidden behind a peak is correctly treated as unreachable, and a symmetric valley resolves left because you check left first.

Hint 4 — verifying the tie-break

Hand-run heights=[2,1,2], K=1 with one drop — trivially it stacks at K. Then run heights=[1,2,1], K=1: both sides are equally low and reachable; your code must raise index 0 (left), giving [2,2,1], not index 2. If you get [1,2,2], your scan is checking right before left.
