Theory
text_formatting_deep_dive_master.html

You are given an elevation map heights where heights[i] is the terrain height at column i. Drop V units of water one at a time from index K. Each unit flows left if it can reach a strictly lower column without climbing a wall; otherwise right by the same rule; otherwise it rises at K. Return the final heights array after all drops.

The twist is entirely in the inner settle rule: lowest reachable is not lowest overall — a valley behind a wall is invisible to the falling drop. Get the reachability constraint and the left-before-right tie-break right, and the rest is a loop.

2   Reading the Problem — insights from the constraints

"One unit at a time": units do not settle simultaneously — each settles completely before the next is dropped, against the terrain as mutated by all earlier drops. The heights array is both input state and output; mutate it in place.
"Eventually fall by moving left": this is reachability, not global minimum. A unit flowing left tracks the lowest column it can reach without first climbing higher than its current footing. The instant the terrain rises, the path is blocked and everything beyond is unreachable.
Left before right, always: the settle precedence is fixed — try the full left scan before the right. This is the source of the left-bias tie-break: when both sides offer an equally low reachable minimum, the left scan wins because it ran first.
Constraints are small (W, V ≤ 100): O(V × W) is comfortably fast. There is no pressure to be clever — the entire difficulty is the precision of the inner settle scan and its tie-breaks.

4   Try It Yourself First

Attempt before reading further. Open hints one at a time.

Hint 1 — the outer shape

With W and V both small, you can afford to fully re-simulate each single drop from scratch. The outer loop runs V times; inside it you settle exactly one unit. The heights array is shared state — mutate it in place.

Hint 2 — the inner settle rule

For one unit dropped at K, try three things in strict order: (1) scan left from K, tracking the lowest reachable index; stop the instant the terrain rises above your current footing. If you found a strictly lower index, settle there. (2) Otherwise scan right by the same rule. (3) Otherwise increment heights[K].

Hint 3 — the reachability scan in detail

Initialize bestIndex = K. Move one column at a time. If the next column is strictly higher than heights[bestIndex], stop — it's a wall. If the next column is strictly lower than heights[bestIndex], update bestIndex. At the end, if bestIndex != K, water settles there. This is not a global-min scan — bestIndex tracks the lowest point reached so far along a descending-or-flat path.

Hint 4 — verifying the tie-break

Run heights=[1,2,1], K=1 with one drop. Your left scan finds index 0 (height 1, lower than K's height 2) — bestIndex becomes 0. Your right scan would also find index 2 (height 1). But left runs first and finds a lower spot, so water settles at index 0. Result: [2,2,1]. If you get [1,2,2], your scan is checking right before left.

5a   Clarifying Questions
Q: "When water can flow equally low to the left and to the right, which side does it settle on?"
A: Left. → I must try the left scan completely before looking right. A symmetric valley always sends water left.
Q: "Can water flow over a taller column to reach a lower valley on the far side?"
A: No — it can only descend or stay level on the way. → My inner scan stops at the first column strictly higher than my current footing.
Q: "Does each unit settle fully before the next is dropped, against the updated terrain?"
A: Yes. → I mutate heights in place and re-scan from K for every single drop.
Q: "Is K always in range, and can K be at the edge of the array?"
A: K is always valid, and can be 0 or W-1. → I need a bounds guard on each side — no left scan when K=0, no right scan when K=W-1.
5b   Say-Out-Loud Plan
"This is a physics simulation — Variant 8.3 in the simulation family. Each unit falls one at a time and settles by a deterministic local rule, so the outer shape is a loop over V drops. The heights array is both the state and the answer; I mutate it in place. The difficulty is entirely in the inner settle rule. The sequence for one drop goes like this: I start a scan leftward from K, keeping track of the lowest column I can reach without the terrain first rising above where I'm standing. The instant a column is taller than my current best, I stop — that's a wall, everything beyond is unreachable. If I found a strictly lower column, I settle there and I'm done. Otherwise I repeat the same scan rightward. If neither side offered a lower reachable spot, I increment heights[K] directly. From that sequence, three pieces of state surface: the heights array itself (mutated in place), a bestIndex variable for the current scan that resets each time I change direction, and loop indices for the outer drop loop and the inner scan. The condition driving the left scan is: while the next column is not higher than heights[bestIndex], keep moving and update bestIndex if the next column is strictly lower. Stop the moment terrain rises. After the scan, settle at bestIndex only if it's strictly lower than heights[K]. Let me trace the canonical example: heights=[2,1,1,2,1,2,2], V=4, K=3, heights[K]=2. Drop 1: scan left from index 2. heights[2]=1 < heights[K]=2 → bestIndex=2. heights[1]=1, not lower than heights[2]=1 → no update. heights[0]=2, higher than heights[1]=1 → stop. bestIndex=2, heights[2]=1 < heights[3]=2 → settle at 2. heights=[2,1,2,2,1,2,2]. Drop 2: scan left from index 2. heights[2]=2, not lower than heights[K]=2 → no update. heights[1]=1 < heights[2]=2 → bestIndex=1. heights[0]=2 > heights[1]=1 → stop. bestIndex=1, heights[1]=1 < heights[3]=2 → settle at 1. heights=[2,2,2,2,1,2,2]. Drop 3: scan left — heights[2]=2, heights[1]=2, heights[0]=2, all equal to heights[K]=2, no lower spot found. Scan right from index 4: heights[4]=1 < heights[K]=2 → bestIndex=4. heights[5]=2 > heights[4]=1 → stop. Settle at 4. heights=[2,2,2,2,2,2,2]. Drop 4: scan left — all equal to heights[K]=2, no lower. Scan right — all equal. In-place: heights[3]+=1. heights=[2,2,2,3,2,2,2]. ✓ Time is O(V × W) — each drop scans at most the full width. Space is O(W) for the mutated heights array, O(1) additional."

6   Approach Selection Rationale

CandidateWhy it loses / ties
Global min scan per dropFinds the globally lowest index, not the reachable one — fails any test with a wall between K and a lower valley. Incorrect, not slow.
Prefix/suffix slice + .min()Loses the index on Array conversion; also scans the whole side without the stop-at-first-rise reachability check. Two bugs in one.
BFS / flood fillWould work but is massive overkill — O(V × W) with a large constant. The linear settle scan is exactly what "descend until blocked" means iteratively.
Reachable-min scan (chosen)Directly models the settle rule: track bestIndex as you walk, stop at first rise, update only on strict descent. O(V × W), correct, minimal state.
The deciding signal: "eventually fall" in the problem statement is a reachability constraint, not a global search. The moment you see "water can't climb a wall," you know it's a local scan that stops at the first rise — not a min() call over a slice.

7   Data Structure Choice

StructureRoleWhy this, not another
[Int] (mutated in place)Terrain + water stateThe problem asks to return the heights array — it IS the answer. No separate accumulator needed. O(1) subscript access per index update.
Int bestIndex scalarTracks the lowest reachable index during each directional scanA single variable is sufficient — the scan is linear and we only care about the running minimum position, not the full path.
ClosedRange<Int> for subrange iterationBounds each directional scan to its valid sideIterating for index in range keeps indices into the original array — no offset arithmetic, no slice copy, no index loss.
Note: Array.min() and firstIndex(of:) are two separate O(W) passes and lose index information on slices. The single-pass scan with bestIndex does it in one pass and preserves the original index throughout.

8   Pseudocode Dry Run

PSEUDOCODELC 755 — Pour Water settle rule
func pourWater(heights, volume, dropIndex):
    for each drop in 1...volume:
        kHeight = heights[dropIndex]

        // Try left
        bestIndex = dropIndex
        if dropIndex > 0:
            for scanIndex from (dropIndex-1) down to 0:
                if heights[scanIndex] > heights[bestIndex]: STOP   ← wall
                if heights[scanIndex] < heights[bestIndex]: bestIndex = scanIndex
        if bestIndex != dropIndex:
            heights[bestIndex] += 1; continue

        // Try right
        bestIndex = dropIndex
        if dropIndex < heights.count - 1:
            for scanIndex from (dropIndex+1) up to (heights.count-1):
                if heights[scanIndex] > heights[bestIndex]: STOP   ← wall
                if heights[scanIndex] < heights[bestIndex]: bestIndex = scanIndex
        if bestIndex != dropIndex:
            heights[bestIndex] += 1; continue

        // Fallback
        heights[dropIndex] += 1

    return heights

9   Swift Solution

SwiftLC 755 — Pour Water · reachable-min settle scan
private func pourWater(_ heights: [Int], _ volume: Int, _ dropIndex: Int) -> [Int] {
    var heightMap = heights

    for _ in 1...volume {
        if !settleLeft(&heightMap, dropIndex) && !settleRight(&heightMap, dropIndex) {
            heightMap[dropIndex] += 1
        }
    }
    return heightMap
}

private func settleLeft(_ heightMap: inout [Int], _ dropIndex: Int) -> Bool {
    guard dropIndex > 0 else { return false }
    var bestIndex = dropIndex
    for scanIndex in stride(from: dropIndex - 1, through: 0, by: -1) {
        if heightMap[scanIndex] > heightMap[bestIndex] { break }
        if heightMap[scanIndex] < heightMap[bestIndex] { bestIndex = scanIndex }
    }
    guard bestIndex != dropIndex else { return false }
    heightMap[bestIndex] += 1
    return true
}

private func settleRight(_ heightMap: inout [Int], _ dropIndex: Int) -> Bool {
    guard dropIndex < heightMap.count - 1 else { return false }
    var bestIndex = dropIndex
    for scanIndex in (dropIndex + 1)...(heightMap.count - 1) {
        if heightMap[scanIndex] > heightMap[bestIndex] { break }
        if heightMap[scanIndex] < heightMap[bestIndex] { bestIndex = scanIndex }
    }
    guard bestIndex != dropIndex else { return false }
    heightMap[bestIndex] += 1
    return true
}
Why two helper functions? The left and right scans are structurally identical except for direction and bounds. Extracting them names the operation ("settle left"), makes the main loop read like the spoken plan, and removes the need for continue after each settle. The inout parameter avoids copying the array on each call.
A.1 Single nested helper — reachable-min via ClosedRange scan
PLAN — Same reachable-min settle rule, different structure. Instead of two top-level inout helpers, a single private inner function getReachableMinIndex(scanning:leftward:) handles both directions. It receives the full range to scan as a ClosedRange<Int> and a leftward flag that reverses iteration order. The caller compares the returned optional index against heights[dropIndex] to decide whether to settle there, making the settle-or-not logic visible in one place.
SwiftLC 755 — Pour Water · A.1 single nested helper
private func pourWater(_ heights: [Int], _ volume: Int, _ dropIndex: Int) -> [Int] {
    var currentHeightMap = heights

    func getReachableMinIndex(scanning range: ClosedRange<Int>, leftward: Bool) -> Int? {
        var bestIndex: Int? = nil
        let indices = leftward ? Array(range.reversed()) : Array(range)
        for index in indices {
            if let currentBest = bestIndex, currentHeightMap[index] > currentHeightMap[currentBest] { break }
            if bestIndex == nil || currentHeightMap[index] < currentHeightMap[bestIndex!] { bestIndex = index }
        }
        return bestIndex
    }

    for _ in 1...volume {
        let kHeight = currentHeightMap[dropIndex]

        let lowestIndexLeft: Int? = dropIndex > 0
            ? getReachableMinIndex(scanning: 0...(dropIndex - 1), leftward: true)
            : nil
        let lowestIndexRight: Int? = dropIndex < currentHeightMap.count - 1
            ? getReachableMinIndex(scanning: (dropIndex + 1)...(currentHeightMap.count - 1), leftward: false)
            : nil

        if let leftIndex = lowestIndexLeft, currentHeightMap[leftIndex] < kHeight {
            currentHeightMap[leftIndex] += 1
        } else if let rightIndex = lowestIndexRight, currentHeightMap[rightIndex] < kHeight {
            currentHeightMap[rightIndex] += 1
        } else {
            currentHeightMap[dropIndex] += 1
        }
    }

    return currentHeightMap
}
Structural difference from the primary solution. The primary solution uses two inout top-level helpers and a short-circuit && chain; this variant consolidates direction handling into one inner function. The tradeoff: the inner function must both scan and pre-build a reversed array (Array(range.reversed())), costing an O(W) allocation per call. The primary solution avoids that by using stride directly. Both are O(V × W) overall; the primary is lighter on allocations.

12   Complexity

VariantTimeSpaceWhy
Reachable-min settle scan (this solution)O(V × W)O(W)V drops × at most W columns scanned per drop. heightMap is O(W); all other state is O(1).
Bottleneck: V × W is unavoidable — each drop requires a scan of potentially the full width, and you must process all V drops. The constraints (V, W ≤ 100) make this ~10,000 ops at worst — negligible.
Amortized note: in practice the left and right scans together cover at most W columns total per drop (left covers 0..K-1, right covers K+1..W-1). So the per-drop cost is W, not 2W, once you split correctly. This is the same savings as observing left + right slices = W.

13   Follow-up Questions

Follow-up 1 — Before you push this to production, can you see any improvements?
The main optimization opportunity is caching the settle position across drops when the terrain is flat. Currently we re-scan from K every drop even when nothing changed. In the worst case (flat terrain, all drops stack at K) we do V full scans and find nothing each time — still O(V × W), but with V × W wasted comparisons. You could short-circuit by remembering "last drop settled here, terrain only changed at that one index" and doing a narrower re-scan. In practice with W, V ≤ 100 this is irrelevant, but at scale it matters.
Follow-up 2 — What if V is very large (millions of drops)?
Binary search on the answer: the total water volume is fixed, so you can determine the final water level mathematically. The terrain fills up like a histogram — binary search on the final water surface height, compute how much volume that level holds, and adjust. O(W log(max_height)) instead of O(V × W). Only correct when water doesn't flow out of bounds.
Follow-up 3 — What if drops can land at different K values?
Each drop is now independent with its own K. The algorithm is unchanged — just replace the fixed dropIndex with the per-drop K from an input array. Still O(V × W) total.
Follow-up 4 — What is LC 42 Trapping Rain Water, and how does it relate?
LC 42 asks how much water the terrain can hold at maximum fill — a prefix/suffix max scan, O(n) time, O(1) space. LC 755 asks where individual drops settle by a local rule — a simulation, O(V × W). Related terrain, completely different questions: 42 is a math problem, 755 is a simulation. See LC42_master.html for the two-pointer approach.
